Section 1

Nipping & Biting

What's actually happening

Puppies explore the world with their mouths. Nipping during play is normal — but it needs to be addressed early. A puppy that learns biting gets a reaction (even a negative one) will keep doing it. And puppy teeth at 12 weeks become adult teeth at 6 months.

What most owners get wrong

Yelping, pulling away dramatically, or pushing the puppy off. All of these are exciting to a puppy — you've just turned biting into a game. The other mistake: letting it slide because "they're just a puppy." They won't grow out of it on their own.

What you can start doing now

Redirect immediately. The moment teeth touch skin, calmly remove your hand and give the puppy something appropriate to chew on (a toy, a chew). If they keep going for your hands, end the interaction — stand up, turn away, disengage. Biting means the fun stops. Be consistent and every person in the household needs to follow the same rule.

When to get help

If your puppy is biting hard enough to break skin regularly, if the biting is paired with growling or stiffening, or if it's getting worse despite consistent redirection. Early intervention prevents this from becoming a real aggression issue as an adult.

Potty Training

What's actually happening

Puppies have small bladders and haven't yet learned where they're supposed to go. Accidents aren't defiance — they're a communication failure. Your puppy doesn't know the rules yet because you haven't made them clear enough.

What most owners get wrong

Giving the puppy too much freedom too soon. A puppy with full access to the house will find a corner to use. The other mistake: punishing accidents after the fact. If you didn't catch them in the act, the moment has passed — your puppy has no idea why you're upset.

What you can start doing now

Restrict freedom and increase frequency. Use the crate when you can't supervise. Take your puppy out every 1–2 hours, immediately after eating, drinking, playing, and waking up. When they go outside, reward immediately — not when you get back inside, right there on the spot. If they don't go within 5 minutes, back to the crate, try again in 20 minutes.

When to get help

If your puppy is over 5 months and still having daily accidents despite consistent routine, or if they're going in their crate regularly. Something else may be going on — a vet check is the first step, then professional training guidance.

Crate Training

What's actually happening

A puppy that can't settle in a crate is a puppy that hasn't learned to be alone. The crate isn't punishment — it's their safe space. But if it was introduced badly (shoved in during a tantrum, used only when you leave), your puppy has already built a negative association.

What most owners get wrong

Only using the crate when they leave the house — so the crate becomes the thing that predicts being alone. The other mistake: letting the puppy out when they cry. You just taught them that crying opens the door.

What you can start doing now

Use the crate while you're home. Feed meals in the crate. Give chews in the crate. Close the door for short periods while you're in the same room. Your puppy needs to learn that the crate is a normal part of daily life — not a signal that you're leaving. Build duration gradually and never let them out while they're fussing.

When to get help

If your puppy is injuring themselves in the crate (broken nails, bloody gums from chewing bars), if they're howling for 30+ minutes without settling, or if crate anxiety is getting worse instead of better. This can escalate into full separation anxiety if not addressed.

Section 4

Recall

What's actually happening

Puppies are naturally curious and easily distracted. When they ignore you, it's not stubbornness — whatever they're doing is simply more rewarding than coming to you. Recall needs to be built from the ground up, and it needs to be enforced every single time so your puppy learns it's not optional.

What most owners get wrong

Calling the puppy only when it's time to leave the park or go in the crate. Your puppy quickly learns that "come" means the fun is over. The other mistake: repeating the command five times. You're teaching them they don't need to respond until you've said it enough.

What you can start doing now

Use high-value treats and a long leash. Call your puppy once. If they come, reward immediately. If they ignore you, use the long leash to guide them back — then reward. The long leash means you can always enforce the recall, so your puppy learns that "come" is not optional. Practice daily in low-distraction environments first.

When to get help

If your puppy has zero recall with any distraction present, or if they're a flight risk (bolting out doors, running away at the park). Don't wait until they're an adult — recall is dramatically easier to build in a puppy than to fix in a grown dog.

Section 5

Socialization

What's actually happening

Puppies have a critical socialization window that starts closing around 14–16 weeks. During this period, their brain is wired to accept new experiences as normal. After the window closes, new things become scary by default. A puppy that wasn't properly socialized is far more likely to develop fear, reactivity, and anxiety as an adult.

What most owners get wrong

Confusing socialization with "meeting lots of dogs." Quality matters more than quantity. Letting a puppy get mobbed at a dog park or overwhelmed by strangers isn't socialization — it's flooding, and it creates the exact fear you're trying to prevent. The other mistake: waiting until they're "fully vaccinated" to start. The socialization window doesn't wait for the vaccine schedule.

What you can start doing now

Expose your puppy to new things daily — at their pace. Different surfaces, sounds, people, environments. Let them observe from a safe distance. Reward calm, confident behaviour. Don't force interactions — let the puppy approach on their terms. A puppy that chooses to investigate is building real confidence. A puppy that's dragged into situations is learning to be afraid.

When to get help

If your puppy is past 14 weeks and showing fear of new people, dogs, or environments. The window is closing and professional guidance will make the most of the time you have left. If your puppy is already highly fearful or reactive, start now — it only gets harder with age.

Section 6

Calmness & Settling

What's actually happening

A puppy that can never settle — constantly following you, pacing, whining, unable to just lie down and relax — has never learned that doing nothing is an option. They're stuck in a loop of needing constant stimulation or attention, and it only gets worse as they grow.

What most owners get wrong

Giving attention every time the puppy demands it. Picking them up when they whine, petting when they nudge, talking to them when they pace. You're rewarding the exact behaviour you want to stop. The other mistake: assuming they'll "calm down with age." Some do. Most don't without being taught.

What you can start doing now

Teach the "place" command. Pick a bed or mat. Lure your puppy onto it with a treat. Say "place." Reward for staying. Start with 10 seconds, build to minutes over days. The goal: your puppy learns that going to their spot and doing nothing earns calm, quiet reward. Also — stop responding to every demand. If your puppy nudges you for attention, ignore it. Reward calm behaviour instead.

When to get help

If your puppy literally cannot settle for any period of time, if the restlessness is paired with anxiety symptoms (panting, drooling, destructive behaviour), or if it's getting worse. A puppy that can't switch off is under chronic stress — and it affects their health and development.

Demanding Behaviour

What's actually happening

Your puppy has learned that barking, whining, pawing, or nudging gets them what they want — whether that's food, attention, play, or access to something. Every time it works, the behaviour gets stronger. You've accidentally trained your puppy to be demanding.

What most owners get wrong

Giving in "just to stop the noise." Even once. That one time teaches your puppy that persistence pays off. They learn to push harder, bark louder, whine longer — because eventually it works. The other mistake: yelling "no" or "stop" — which is still attention, and attention is what they wanted.

What you can start doing now

Completely ignore demanding behaviour. No eye contact, no talking, no touching. Wait for even 2 seconds of quiet or calm, then reward that. You're teaching your puppy that being calm gets them what they want — not barking. Everyone in the household needs to be on the same page. One person giving in undoes everyone else's work.

When to get help

If the demanding behaviour has escalated to snapping, resource guarding, or aggressive outbursts when your puppy doesn't get what they want. That's no longer just annoying — it's a behaviour that will become a serious problem as an adult if not corrected now.
