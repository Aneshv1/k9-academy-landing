Section 1

Leash Walking

What's actually happening

Your dog is not being "bad." They are simply more motivated by what is ahead of them than by what is beside them — you. Pulling works. Every time they pull and reach the thing they want, they learn that pulling = reward.

What most owners get wrong

Letting your dog pull "just this once." Inconsistency is the number one reason leash training fails. If pulling works sometimes, your dog will keep trying.

One thing to try today

The moment your dog pulls, stop walking. Don't yank them back. Just stop. Wait until there is slack in the leash, then continue. If they pull again, stop again. Your walk will be painfully slow the first few days. By day 4 or 5, most dogs start to get it — pulling means we stop, loose leash means we go.

When to get help

If your dog is over 40 lbs and physically dragging you, or if they redirect aggression when corrected on leash, stop trying to fix this alone. You need a trainer who can introduce the right tools safely.

Section 2

Reactivity

What's actually happening

Reactivity is almost always rooted in fear or frustration — not aggression. Your dog sees another dog and their nervous system fires. They bark, lunge, or growl because they've learned that making noise makes the scary thing go away.

What most owners get wrong

Comforting your dog when they react. "It's okay, buddy" reinforces the state they're in. Also: avoiding triggers entirely means your dog never learns to handle them.

One thing to try today

Set firm leash rules for the entire walk — not just when you see another dog. If you let your dog sniff everything, pull you from tree to tree, and walk wherever they want, you can't expect them to suddenly listen when a dog appears. Structure the walk first. Your dog walks beside you, on a loose leash, focused on you. Once the walk has rules, reactivity becomes much easier to address.

When to get help

If your dog is reacting at 50+ feet, if they have ever made contact with another dog or person, or if you feel physically unsafe on walks. Reactivity escalates without intervention.

Section 3

Off-Leash Recall

What's actually happening

Your dog has learned that whatever they're doing is more rewarding than coming back to you. Recall is not about obedience — it's about being more interesting than the environment.

What most owners get wrong

Only calling your dog when it's time to leave the park. Your dog learns that "come" means "fun is over." Also: repeating the command. If you say "come" five times, your dog learns they don't need to respond until the fifth.

One thing to try today

Use high-value treats (cheese, hot dog, chicken — something your dog goes crazy for) and a long leash. Call your dog once. If they come, reward immediately. If they ignore you, use the long leash to guide them back — then reward. The long leash means you can always enforce the recall, so your dog learns that "come" is not optional.

When to get help

If your dog has zero recall around distractions, or if they're a flight risk. Off-leash reliability requires structured e-collar work in most cases — that needs a professional.

Section 4

Separation Tolerance

What's actually happening

Separation anxiety is not your dog being dramatic. It is a genuine panic response. Their nervous system cannot regulate without you present. Common signs: howling, destruction at exits, panting, pacing, refusing to eat.

What most owners get wrong

Making departures and arrivals a big deal. Long goodbyes and excited greetings teach your dog that your leaving and returning are significant events.

What you can start doing now

Stop over-coddling your dog. Constantly carrying them, letting them follow you into every room, never letting them be alone — this builds dependence, not confidence. Your dog needs to learn that being alone is normal and safe, not something to panic about.

Leave your dog alone regularly — starting young. If you have a puppy, make alone time a daily habit from day one. Crate them for short periods while you're home. Leave the house without making it a big deal. Build independence before anxiety has a chance to form.

But if it's already a problem — get help

True separation anxiety is not a DIY fix. The internet is full of tips like "practice boring departures" or "desensitize to your keys" — in our experience, they don't work. They waste your time and delay the real fix.

Separation anxiety is a panic response that needs to be addressed directly with structured training. The longer it goes unaddressed, the worse it gets. If your dog is howling, destroying things, or panicking when you leave — call us.

Settling at Home

What's actually happening

A dog that cannot settle is a dog with no off switch. They follow you room to room, whine when you sit down, demand attention constantly. This is often because the dog was never taught that doing nothing is an option.

What most owners get wrong

Giving attention every time the dog demands it. Petting when they nudge you, talking when they whine. You're rewarding the behaviour you want to stop.

One thing to try today

The "place" command. Pick a bed or mat. Lure your dog onto it with a treat. Say "place." Reward them for staying. Start with 10 seconds, build to minutes. This is one of the most transformative commands you can teach.

When to get help

If your dog's inability to settle is paired with anxiety symptoms, or if they're reactive to sounds and movement inside the home. A dog that cannot relax is under chronic stress.

Section 6

Aggression

What's actually happening

Aggression can look scary, but it's rarely random. Whether it's rooted in fear, territorial instinct, or resource guarding, there's always a reason behind it — and with the right approach, it can be addressed. The key is not to ignore it or hope it goes away, because it won't.

What most owners get wrong

Working around the problem instead of addressing it. Avoiding certain situations, keeping your dog away from triggers, rearranging your routine — these feel like solutions but they don't change the underlying behaviour. Your dog needs clear structure, consistent rules, and guidance on what's expected of them.

What you can start doing now

Add more structure to your dog's daily life. Clear rules, consistent boundaries, and calm leadership make a real difference. Dogs with aggression issues often improve when they understand what's expected of them.

For resource guarding: don't leave high-value items lying around for the dog to claim and protect. If your dog guards toys, bones, or food — manage access to those resources. You decide when they're available.

This is one to get help with early

Aggression tends to get worse over time if it's not addressed. The sooner you work with a professional, the easier and faster the fix. Your dog needs to learn proper socialization while understanding that aggressive behaviour isn't the answer.
