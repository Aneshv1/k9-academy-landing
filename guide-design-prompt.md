### Page 2 — How to use this guide
You just completed the K9 Academy behaviour assessment. This guide breaks down the 6 areas we tested, explains what is actually going on with your dog, and gives you one thing you can try today for each.

This is not a replacement for professional training. It is a starting point — and for some dogs, it will be enough to see immediate improvement. For others, it will show you exactly why professional help is the next step.

### Page 3 — Section 1: LEASH WALKING

**What is actually happening when your dog pulls**
Your dog is not being "bad." They are simply more motivated by what is ahead of them than by what is beside them — you. Pulling works for them. Every time they pull and get to the thing they want (a smell, another dog, the park), they learn that pulling = reward.

**What most owners get wrong**
Letting your dog pull "just this once." Inconsistency is the number one reason leash training fails. If pulling works sometimes, your dog will keep trying.

**One thing to try today**
The moment your dog pulls, stop walking. Do not yank them back. Just stop. Wait until there is slack in the leash, then continue. If they pull again, stop again. Your walk will be painfully slow the first few days. By day 4 or 5, most dogs start to get it — pulling means we stop, loose leash means we go.

**When to get help**
If your dog is over 40 lbs and physically dragging you, or if they redirect aggression when corrected on leash, stop trying to fix this alone. You need a trainer who can introduce the right tools safely.

### Page 4 — Section 2: REACTIVITY

**What is actually happening when your dog reacts**
Reactivity is almost always rooted in fear or frustration — not aggression. Your dog sees another dog (or person, bike, etc.) and their nervous system fires. They bark, lunge, or growl because they have learned that making noise makes the scary thing go away (the other dog's owner crosses the street, you turn around, etc.).

**What most owners get wrong**
Comforting your dog when they react. "It is okay, buddy" reinforces the state they are in. You are telling them that reacting is the correct response. Also: avoiding triggers entirely means your dog never learns to handle them.

**One thing to try today**
Create distance. When you see a trigger approaching, calmly change direction before your dog reacts. The goal is to keep them under threshold — the point where they notice but have not lost it yet. Reward calm behaviour at that distance. Over days, gradually decrease the distance.

**When to get help**
If your dog is reacting at 50+ feet, if they have ever made contact with another dog or person, or if you feel physically unsafe on walks. Reactivity escalates without intervention — it does not get better on its own.

### Page 5 — Section 3: OFF-LEASH RECALL

**What is actually happening when your dog ignores you**
Your dog has learned that whatever they are doing (chasing a squirrel, playing with another dog) is more rewarding than coming back to you. Recall is not about obedience — it is about being more interesting than the environment.

**What most owners get wrong**
Only calling your dog when it is time to leave the park. Your dog learns that "come" means "fun is over." They stop coming. Also: repeating the command. If you say "come" five times, your dog learns they do not need to respond until the fifth one.

**One thing to try today**
Practice recall at home first — zero distractions. Say your dog's name once, and when they look at you, reward immediately (treat, toy, praise). Do this 10 times a day for a week. You are building the reflex: my name = something good happens. Do not try this off leash in public until it is reliable in your house and backyard.

**When to get help**
If your dog has zero recall around distractions, or if they are a flight risk (bolts out doors, runs from you at the park). Off-leash reliability requires structured e-collar work in most cases — and that needs to be introduced by a professional, not a YouTube video.

### Page 6 — Section 4: SEPARATION TOLERANCE

**What is actually happening when your dog panics alone**
Separation anxiety is not your dog being dramatic. It is a genuine panic response. Their nervous system cannot regulate without you present. Common signs: howling, destruction (specifically at exits — doors, windows, crates), panting, pacing, refusing to eat.

**What most owners get wrong**
Making departures and arrivals a big deal. Long goodbyes and excited greetings teach your dog that your leaving and returning are significant events. Also: crating a dog with severe separation anxiety without desensitization — this often makes it worse.

**One thing to try today**
Practice "boring departures." Pick up your keys, put on your shoes, then sit back down. Do this 10 times. Open the front door, step out for 3 seconds, come back in. No greeting. Do this 10 times. You are desensitizing your dog to departure cues. Slowly increase the duration over days and weeks.

**When to get help**
If your dog is injuring themselves (breaking out of crates, chewing through doors, breaking windows), if your building has noise complaints, or if the behaviour has lasted more than a month. Separation anxiety is one of the hardest issues to fix alone — most owners need professional guidance to get it right.

### Page 7 — Section 5: SETTLING AT HOME

**What is actually happening when your dog will not relax**
A dog that cannot settle is a dog with no off switch. They follow you room to room, whine when you sit down, demand attention constantly, or pace and pant for no apparent reason. This is often a result of the dog never being taught that doing nothing is an option.

**What most owners get wrong**
Giving attention every time the dog demands it. Petting them when they nudge you, talking to them when they whine, letting them on the couch every time they jump up. You are rewarding the behaviour you want to stop.

**One thing to try today**
The "place" command. Pick a bed or mat. Lure your dog onto it with a treat. Say "place." Reward them for staying on it. Start with 10 seconds, then 30, then a minute. Build up over days. The goal: your dog learns that going to their place and doing nothing earns calm, quiet reward. This is one of the most transformative commands you can teach.

**When to get help**
If your dog's inability to settle is paired with anxiety symptoms (panting, drooling, destructive behaviour), or if they are reactive to sounds and movement inside the home. A dog that cannot relax is a dog under chronic stress — and that affects their health, not just your sanity.

### Page 8 — Section 6: AGGRESSION

**What is actually happening when your dog shows aggression**
Aggression is almost never random. It is a response to fear, pain, resource value, or territorial instinct. Growling, stiffening, snapping, and biting are communication — your dog is saying "I am uncomfortable and I need this to stop." The problem is that these signals escalate if they are not addressed.

**What most owners get wrong**
Punishing growling. A growl is a warning. If you punish the warning, you still have a dog that is uncomfortable — they just stop warning you before they bite. Also: assuming your dog will "grow out of it." Aggression escalates without intervention.

**One thing to try today**
If you can identify the trigger (strangers at the door, resource guarding food, being touched in a certain spot), manage the environment to prevent the situation from occurring while you seek professional help. For resource guarding: trade up. Approach with something better than what they have. Do not take things away — offer an exchange.

**When to get help**
Immediately, if your dog has made contact (bitten) or if the aggression is escalating in frequency or intensity. This is not something to work through with online tips. You need a trainer who specializes in behaviour modification and will assess your dog in person. We work with dogs other trainers have turned down — including dogs with bite history.

### Page 9 — What your score means

**15–18: Your dog is ahead of the pack.**
You have a solid foundation. Targeted group classes or a tune-up session can take you from good to great — especially if off-leash reliability is the goal.

**10–14: Room for improvement.**
Your dog has some clear gaps. The good news: these are very fixable with the right approach. Private training or group classes will get you there.

**6–9: Your dog needs professional help.**
You are dealing with multiple behavioural issues that are reinforcing each other. This is not a DIY situation. Board and train or intensive private sessions are the fastest path to a dog you can trust.
